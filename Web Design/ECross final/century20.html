The 20th Century: The Progressive Era

The 1900s were known as The Progressive Era. Cities offer many jobs in factories and administrative departments. The manufacturing business created a new staple of the American economy. The rapidly growing and expanding industrial labor market lured immigrants from other countries as well as others looking for work. As industrialization continued to grow and advance, so did the development of urban settlements in the cities. A large surge of American citizens and immigrants alike relocated from rural farm areas closer to the cities. While many people who took residence in the city stayed in deplorable and over-populated living conditions, the era was still considered an “Age of Great Prosperity”.

Unfortunately, wall street crashed in October 1929 and so began the collapse the foundation of the economy. One result was a sharp halt on industrial manufacturing, had been a dominating sector in the American economy. As production steeply declined, the unemployment levels dramatically rose. People no longer had a means to financially maintain their homes and so there was an onset of national epidemic of homelessness. A new form of human settlements called Hooverville began to appear frequently across the nation. According the PBS documentary, "America 1900", the term Hooverville was popularized after the U.S. President, Herbert Hoover, who was greatly blamed for the cause of The Great Depression. The term was used to describe squatter or informal living areas. Hundreds of "Hoovervilles" were created across the nation during the 1930s where thousands of people occupied these shanty areas.

The economy eventually recovered, however, The Great Depression officially lasted for ten devastating years. The Great Depression era remains known as the most devastating and longest-lasting economic plummet in the history of the United States.
