Effects of North American Settlements in Nature: The Disappearance of the Passenger Pigeon

Memorial statue of Martha, the last known Passenger Pigeon
© Smithsonian Institution Archives

The establishment and advancement of European settlements has been both positive and negative. Many adverse effects have results not only for non-European settlers but also for the environment. The needs of European settlers has been great and requiring much the environment’s natural resources. However, these requirements has left he environment adversely molded.

Once such noted effect is the disappearance of the North American Passenger pigeon. In North American, Passenger pigeons used to be the most abundant bird. However, the species was over hunted as a food source and their habitats were destroyed. This led to a sharp decline in the population of Passenger pigeons and ultimately their extinction. By 1900, scientist had deemed the population as nearly extinct. The Passenger pigeon known as “Martha”, was the last known living Passenger pigeon. On September 1, 1914, the Passenger pigeon known as Martha, died. With the death of this bird, the specie known as the Passenger Pigeon would officially be considered extinct.
